How Much Does a Walk-In Shower Cost? (2025 Complete Guide)

James Crawford Home Renovation Specialist
March 18, 2026
Ad · Leaderboard
✓ Key Takeaways
  • ✓Average walk-in shower costs $3,500–$15,000 installed
  • ✓Size is the #1 cost driver — each extra foot adds $800–$1,500
  • ✓Waterproofing is where contractors cut corners — always ask
  • ✓Get 3+ quotes; a suspiciously low bid usually means hidden costs

The average walk-in shower installation costs $3,500 to $15,000, with most homeowners spending around $6,800 for a standard 36×36-inch tiled shower with a frameless glass door. What you pay depends heavily on size, materials, and whether you're converting an existing tub or starting fresh.

Walk-In Shower Cost by Size

Size is the single biggest cost driver. Larger showers require more tile, more waterproofing membrane, more glass, and more labor hours. Here's what you can expect at each common footprint:

A 32×32-inch shower — the smallest practical size — typically costs $2,800 to $5,500 installed. You'll find these in powder rooms being converted or tight en-suite bathrooms where every inch counts. The savings come from less tile and a smaller glass panel, but the labor cost doesn't scale down proportionally.

At 36×48 inches, the sweet spot for most remodels, costs run $4,500 to $8,500. This size feels genuinely comfortable for daily use without pushing into luxury territory. Most prefab shower kits are designed around this footprint, which can cut costs if you're open to non-custom options.

  • 32×32 inches — $2,800 to $5,500 installed
  • 36×36 inches — $3,500 to $7,000 installed (most common)
  • 36×48 inches — $4,500 to $8,500 installed
  • 48×48 inches — $6,000 to $12,000 installed
  • 60×36 walk-in wet room — $8,000 to $18,000 installed

Cost by Material: Tile vs Prefab vs Stone

Material choice affects both the upfront cost and the long-term maintenance burden. Ceramic tile is the industry default — it's affordable, waterproof, available in thousands of styles, and repairable if a single tile cracks. Porcelain tile costs more but handles moisture better and lasts longer without staining.

Natural stone — marble, slate, travertine — adds a significant premium. Expect to pay $25 to $65 per square foot for the stone alone, plus additional labor because stone requires more precise cutting and sealing. It's stunning, but it needs annual resealing to stay waterproof and can crack if the subfloor shifts.

Prefab shower kits, by contrast, can bring the total project cost under $2,500 if you're doing a straight swap. For more on what drives bathroom costs overall, see our guide on bathroom remodel costs by project type.

In 15 years of renovation work, I've seen more walk-in shower projects go over budget than almost any other job. The hidden cost is almost always waterproofing — contractors who cut corners on the membrane create problems that show up 3 years later as water damage behind the walls.

— James Crawford, Home Renovation Specialist

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I convert a bathtub to a walk-in shower myself?

The demolition is DIY-able, but the waterproofing, tile work, and plumbing connections require licensed contractors in most jurisdictions. Cutting corners on waterproofing leads to water damage that can cost more than the original shower to fix.

Does a walk-in shower add value to a home?

Yes — in most markets, a well-done walk-in shower recoups 60–70% of its cost at resale. The ROI is highest when you're adding a shower to a bathroom that currently only has a tub, especially in master suites.

How long does installation take?

A standard conversion takes 3–5 days for a professional crew. Custom tile work with intricate patterns or large-format tiles can extend the timeline to 7–10 days. The tile mortar and grout need curing time before the shower is usable.

Ad · In-Article 300×250

The Bottom Line

A walk-in shower conversion costs $3,500 to $15,000 for most homeowners, with the biggest variables being size, tile choice, and glass door style. Get at least three quotes from licensed contractors, ask specifically about what's included in their waterproofing process, and budget a 15% contingency for surprises behind the walls.

The difference between a shower that looks great for 5 years and one that looks great for 25 is almost entirely in the waterproofing and the quality of the tile installation. Don't optimize that part of the budget.

About James Crawford

James Crawford grew up watching his father renovate the family's Victorian-era farmhouse outside Columbus, Ohio — a project that spanned most of his childhood and left him with a deep respect for both the craft and the cost of doing things right. By the time he was fourteen, he could frame a doorway, hang drywall, and quote a materials list from memory. That early education shaped everything that followed.

After earning a construction management degree from Ohio State University in 2004, James joined a mid-size regional contractor that specialized in residential renovations. For the next decade he worked on projects ranging from single-bathroom updates to full gut-renovations of historic properties, eventually leading crews of up to twelve people. He earned his general contractor's license in 2009 and his certified remodeler designation through the National Association of the Remodeling Industry in 2011.

The pivot toward consumer advocacy came from frustration. James watched too many homeowners get burned by vague estimates, hidden scope changes, and contractors who priced jobs below cost and then made up the margin in change orders. In 2015, he began writing a column for a regional home improvement magazine explaining how to read a contractor's bid, what questions to ask before signing, and how to spot the warning signs of a problematic job.

The response was immediate. Readers flooded the magazine's inbox with questions, and James realized the real gap wasn't in how-to content — the internet had plenty of that — but in honest, specific cost information. What does a bathroom remodel actually cost in a mid-size Midwest city? What's the markup on materials? When is a quote genuinely competitive and when is it suspiciously low?

He joined The Daily Pulse in 2022 to focus on exactly that: transparent, contractor-vetted cost guides that help homeowners negotiate from a position of knowledge rather than hope. Every figure he publishes is cross-referenced against current supplier pricing, regional labor data, and conversations with active contractors across multiple markets.

Outside of writing, James still takes on select renovation projects through his consultancy, which keeps him current on what materials actually cost and what good work genuinely requires. He lives in Columbus with his wife and two kids, and is currently, inevitably, renovating his own kitchen.
